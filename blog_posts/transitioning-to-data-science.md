# Transitioning from Systems Verification to Data Science: My 12-Year Journey
Date: July 14, 2026
Category: Personal Reflection
Description: A deep dive into my professional shift from writing automated chipset verification scripts at Qualcomm to engineering production data science pipelines.

For over five years, my day-to-day work was focused on hardware systems engineering. At Qualcomm, I wrote automated Python scripts to verify mobile chipsets. It was precision work: monitoring voltages, debugging signal timing, and parsing high-volume log streams to detect chip design bugs.

But over time, I noticed that the most interesting part of my job wasn't just finding the bugs—it was analyzing the *data* generated by the test beds. We had gigabytes of telemetry records detailing power curves and thermal drops. I began writing pandas models to cluster chipset failure modes, and that was the spark that ignited my shift into Data Science.

### The Commonalities Between Hardware Testing & Data Pipelines
Many engineers view hardware testing and data science as completely separate fields, but their underlying architectures are identical:
1. **Pipeline Automation:** In chipset testing, you automate data collection loops. In ETL pipelines, you build identical orchestration loops (e.g. Azure Data Factory or AWS Glue).
2. **Quality Gates:** Finding a silicon bug is exactly like finding an outlier in an ML training dataset. It requires rigorous statistical validation.
3. **Performance Optimization:** Debugging low-level driver execution times translates directly to writing efficient, optimized SQL queries and indexing databases.

### Moving Forward
By taking the systems-level rigor of hardware engineering and applying it to machine learning, I've built a unique analytical approach. I don't just train models; I build the pipelines that feed them, scale them inside cloud infrastructures, and design the dashboards that explain their value.

This engineering blog is my notebook. In the coming weeks, you will see automated case studies detailing my ETL architectures, demand forecasts, and SQL query optimizations. Thanks for reading!
